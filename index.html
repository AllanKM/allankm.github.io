The work

Every serious change starts the same way: a business outcome that the current estate cannot deliver cleanly—new products across markets, lower cost to serve, risk the board will not accept, or a platform that has become too fragile to move.

My role is to hold that outcome long enough to turn it into a target shape for the enterprise: which capabilities must exist, which systems own them, what can be shared, what must stay separate, and what sequence of change the organization can actually absorb. Architecture here is not a catalogue of components. It is the agreement between business and IT on how value will be created and protected.

I have spent more than twenty years in that space, mostly in banking, insurance, and logistics. The thread is constant: sit with the business problem, make the trade-offs visible, govern the decisions, and stay with the programme until the operating model and the systems line up.

How I practice

  • Business → capability — start from outcomes and operating model; map to capabilities and ownership before choosing solutions.
  • Options and trade-offs — rebuild, buy, reuse, or migrate—with cost, risk, time, and organizational readiness on the same page.
  • Governance — principles, decision records, and review that keep programmes coherent when many teams deliver in parallel.
  • Transformation sequencing — design a path that reduces downtime and cost while the business keeps running on the estate being changed.
  • Leadership of the architecture conversation — align executives, product, risk, and delivery on one story of the target state.

Selected outcomes

Industry narratives (organizations omitted).

  • Multi-market insurance product platform — shaped a shared platform so group companies could sell and operate on common capabilities instead of rebuilding per country.
  • Estate modernization under load — led the business case and target architecture for moving critical insurance workloads off a fragile legacy path; roughly 25% lower operating cost and 40% less downtime where the change landed, with faster release of business change.
  • Cross-domain integration — aligned logistics, support, billing, and partners into a coherent integration landscape so the business could see one process instead of many system handoffs.
  • Productized delivery — established architecture and review practice so SaaS-style offerings could ship repeatedly without each release reinventing the estate.
